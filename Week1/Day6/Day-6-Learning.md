### CORE RULE 1: DO / DOES / DID - For Questions and Negatives

**Explanation:**
"Do", "does", and "did" are auxiliary (helping) verbs used for:
- Making questions in simple tenses
- Making negative sentences
- Emphasizing actions

**Rules:**
- **DO** = I, you, we, they (present)
- **DOES** = he, she, it (present)
- **DID** = all subjects (past)

**Structure:**
- Questions: Do/Does/Did + subject + base verb?
- Negatives: Subject + do/does/did + not + base verb
- Emphasis: Subject + do/does/did + base verb

**10 Examples:**

1. **I do automation testing every day.**
   (Emphasis on "do" - I really do it)

2. **He doesn't know Python.**
   (Negative with "does" for he/she/it)

3. **Do you have experience with Selenium?**
   (Question with "do" for you/we/they)

4. **She does manual testing sometimes.**
   (Emphasis for she/he/it)

5. **They don't work on weekends.**
   (Negative with "do" for they/we)

6. **Did you fix the bug yesterday?**
   (Question in past - "did" for all subjects)

7. **I didn't attend the meeting.**
   (Negative past - "didn't" + base verb)

8. **Does this test case cover all scenarios?**
   (Question with "does" for it/he/she)

9. **We do follow Agile methodology.**
   (Emphasis - we really do follow it)

10. **He didn't complete the automation on time.**
    (Negative past for he)

**Common Mistake Indians Make:**
‚ùå "He don't know Selenium"
‚úÖ "He doesn't know Selenium"

‚ùå "She don't have experience"
‚úÖ "She doesn't have experience"

‚ùå "Do he work here?"
‚úÖ "Does he work here?"

‚ùå "I didn't worked on that project"
‚úÖ "I didn't work on that project" (did + base verb, NOT did + past tense)

**Why?** In Hindi/Telugu, we don't change the auxiliary based on subject. We say "woh nahi jaanta" for both he/they. English requires "does" for he/she/it.

**Memory Trick:**
**"HE/SHE/IT = DOES | EVERYONE ELSE = DO | PAST = DID (for all)"**

**Quick Reference:**
- I do, I don't, Do I?
- You do, You don't, Do you?
- He/She/It does, He/She/It doesn't, Does he/she/it?
- We do, We don't, Do we?
- They do, They don't, Do they?
- ALL subjects did, ALL didn't, Did ALL?

### CORE RULE 2: HAVE / HAS / HAD - For Possession and Present Perfect

**Explanation:**
"Have", "has", and "had" are used for:
- Possession (having something)
- Present Perfect tense
- Experiences

**Rules:**
- **HAVE** = I, you, we, they (present)
- **HAS** = he, she, it (present)
- **HAD** = all subjects (past)

**10 Examples:**

1. **I have 6 years of experience in automation.**
   (Possession - I possess experience)

2. **She has excellent testing skills.**
   (Possession for she/he/it)

3. **We have a meeting at 3 PM.**
   (Possession - we possess a scheduled meeting)

4. **He has worked on multiple projects.**
   (Present Perfect for he/she/it)

5. **They have completed the regression testing.**
   (Present Perfect for they/we)

6. **I had a bug in my code yesterday.**
   (Past possession)

7. **She had worked at Infosys before joining TCS.**
   (Past Perfect)

8. **Do you have experience with Playwright?**
   (Question using "do have")

9. **It has been a challenging week.**
   (Present Perfect for it)

10. **We had finished testing before the deployment.**
    (Past Perfect)

**Common Mistake Indians Make:**
‚ùå "She have 5 years of experience"
‚úÖ "She has 5 years of experience"

‚ùå "He have knowledge of Python"
‚úÖ "He has knowledge of Python"

‚ùå "I have went to the office" (have + past tense)
‚úÖ "I have gone to the office" (have + past participle)

‚ùå "She don't have experience"
‚úÖ "She doesn't have experience"

**Why?** We use one form for all subjects in our languages. English changes "have" to "has" for he/she/it.

**Memory Trick:**
**"HE/SHE/IT HAS | EVERYONE ELSE HAVE | PAST = HAD (all)"**

**Quick Reference:**
- I have, I don't have, I have done
- You have, You don't have, You have done
- He/She/It has, He/She/It doesn't have, He/She/It has done
- We have, We don't have, We have done
- They have, They don't have, They have done
- ALL subjects had, ALL didn't have, ALL had done

### CORE RULE 3: IS / ARE / WAS / WERE - "To Be" Verb

**Explanation:**
"Is", "are", "was", "were" are forms of "to be" used for:
- Describing states (I am happy, he is busy)
- Continuous tenses (I am working, they were testing)
- Passive voice (It is done, they were tested)

**Rules:**
**Present:**
- **AM** = I (only)
- **IS** = he, she, it
- **ARE** = you, we, they

**Past:**
- **WAS** = I, he, she, it
- **WERE** = you, we, they

**10 Examples:**

1. **I am a QA Automation Engineer.**
   (Present state - I)

2. **He is working on the test framework.**
   (Present continuous for he/she/it)

3. **They are testing the new feature.**
   (Present continuous for they/we/you)

4. **The application is running on the server.**
   (Present state for it)

5. **We are responsible for quality assurance.**
   (Present state for we/they)

6. **I was testing the application yesterday.**
   (Past continuous - I)

7. **She was the lead QA in her previous company.**
   (Past state for she/he/it)

8. **They were debugging the issue all day.**
   (Past continuous for they/we/you)

9. **The tests were failing due to a configuration error.**
   (Past state for plural)

10. **You were right about that bug.**
    (Past state for you - always "were")

**Common Mistake Indians Make:**
‚ùå "He are a tester"
‚úÖ "He is a tester"

‚ùå "They was testing yesterday"
‚úÖ "They were testing yesterday"

‚ùå "I is working on automation"
‚úÖ "I am working on automation"

‚ùå "She are good at coding"
‚úÖ "She is good at coding"

‚ùå "You was at the meeting"
‚úÖ "You were at the meeting" (You is ALWAYS "are/were", never "is/was")

**Why?** We don't conjugate "to be" in our languages the same way. We might say "woh hai" for both "he is" and "they are".

**Memory Trick:**
**"I AM, YOU ARE, HE/SHE/IT IS, WE/THEY ARE"**
**"I/HE/SHE/IT WAS, YOU/WE/THEY WERE"**

**Quick Reference:**
Present: I am, You are, He/She/It is, We are, They are
Past: I was, You were, He/She/It was, We were, They were
Negative: I'm not, You aren't, He/She/It isn't, We aren't, They aren't
Question: Am I?, Are you?, Is he/she/it?, Are we?, Are they?
